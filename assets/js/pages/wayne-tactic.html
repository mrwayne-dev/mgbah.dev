01 Tactical Identity

A vertical positional team that attacks asymmetrically and defends compactly.

Short, vertical passing. Quick transitions. We create overloads on the left. Rex our play-maker on the right. A back four that becomes a back three. A left-back who attacks space forward.

Style
Vertical Positional Play. Short, sharp passes through Ibiso; the ball moves forward with intent, not sideways for show.
Possession Shape
3-2-5, a back three plus a double pivot supporting a true front five across all five vertical lanes.
Out of Possession
4-4-2 mid-block, semi-high line, sat 15-20m from our box. We invite, we don’t chase.
Core Principle
Attack with five. Defend with five. Discipline is what makes the structure work.
Goal Source
Overloads, late arrivals, exploitation of the five-lane structure. There is no fixed striker, goals come from anywhere in the front five plus arriving runners.
Defensive Identity
Compact. Maximum 10 metres between midfield and back lines at all times. We close space inside the block, we don’t reach for it.
02 The Two Shapes

One team. Two shapes. Continuous transition.

On the team-sheet we are 4-4-2. On the ball we become 3-2-5. The transformation runs through two moves: Mill tucking as a third centre-back, and Goody pushing high from left-back into the front five. Toggle between shapes below.

04 Build-Up Sequence

From Goalkeeper to final third in five touches.

The standard build-up funnel. Every pass has a purpose, not sideways for show, but vertical via Ibiso into Rex or Fortune. Mill never receives from the keeper; he is a defensive anchor in build-up, not an outlet.

01
GK
Plays short to Etienne or Gabi. Never long, never to Mill.
02
Etienne / Gabi
Receives. Holds. Draws the press. Looks for Ibiso between the lines.
03
Ibiso
Drops before the pass arrives. Receives, turns, plays forward instantly.
04
Rex / Fortune
Rex from the right half-space, or Fortune as central link. The pivot of the attack.
05
Bobby / Jobi
Final third. Left overload activates, or Jobi runs at his man.
Key timing Ibiso must drop as the opposition striker commits to pressing the CB, not after receiving the pass. The movement comes first. Late drops are how high presses get bypassed by the opposition, not by us.
05 Primary Overload

The left side 2v1.

The core attacking mechanism. Bobby and Goody together against the opposition right back. He cannot mark both. We force the choice. Then we punish whichever choice he makes.

Left 2v1, Bobby + Goody
Two opposite runs, never the same direction.
Option A: overlap Option B: inside Late arrival
How it works
  • Bobby occupies the opposition right-back wide.
  • Goody runs opposite to Bobby, if Bobby holds wide, Goody runs inside or overlaps.
  • The right-back cannot mark both at once.
  • Ibiso feeds the left from the pivot.
  • Fortune times his run into the box. Rex drifts central for the cutback.
The interchange rule Bobby wide → Goody inside run.
Bobby cuts inside → Goody makes the overlap.
They always move in opposite directions. Never the same run simultaneously.
Decision tree

RB tight on Bobby → pass to Goody behind. Goody crosses or cuts back.

RB commits to Goody → Bobby cuts inside, attacks the half-space, shoots or finds late runner.

06 Secondary Threat

Rex in the right half-space.

The release valve. Rex drops between the opposition’s LM and LB, a zone defenders won’t track strikers into. He receives, scans, and chooses: switch the play, slide Jobi in behind, or combine with Fortune. Then he keeps running.

Rex’s three options
Switch · Thru · Combo, then keep running.
Why this is hard to defend

In the OOP shape Rex is a striker. Defenders marking him in their 4-4-2 don’t track him when he drops. He marks himself off by dropping into a zone defenders don’t follow strikers into.

Rex is the trigger When Rex receives in the right half-space, the rest of the front line moves:
Bobby makes his run · Fortune times his entry · Goody checks his overlap · Jobi demands the pass.
Everyone moves on Rex receiving. Not before.
Rex’s job, in order
  1. 01   Drop from front 5 to right half-space
  2. 02   Receive with time and space
  3. 03   Read the picture
  4. 04   Play the right pass
  5. 05   Continue the run into the box
07 The Front 5

Five players. Five different jobs.

No fixed striker. Five distinct roles that combine into one structure. Each role has a phase-1 (build-up) and phase-2 (final third) responsibility.

Bobby
● Wide Forward · Left Channel

Stays wide. Hugs the touchline in possession. Bobby is the primary goal threat from open play. He doesn’t need to create, he finishes what others create.

His most important movement is the interchange with Goody. When Goody overlaps, Bobby cuts inside to attack the box. When Goody runs inside, Bobby attacks the space behind the right back.

Positioning rule Bobby is always the widest player on the left. If Goody goes wide, Bobby goes inside. Never both wide at once.
Goody
● Advanced Wing-Back · Left Half-Space

The attacking full-back who becomes a forward in possession. Two movement options: overlap (run behind Bobby) or underlap (cut inside). He reads Bobby’s movement and does the opposite, every time.

After delivering or receiving, gets into the box as a third arriving attacker. Critical OOP duty: recovers to right-back the moment possession is lost.

The Goody-Ibiso dependency Goody can only push high when Ibiso is in a covering position behind him. If Ibiso has gone forward, Goody holds. Non-negotiable.
Fortune
● Shadow Striker · Centre

Phase 1 (build-up): Holds with back to goal between midfield and the final third. Draws defenders. Links Rex → Fortune → Rex (give-and-go) or lays off to Ibiso.

Phase 2 (final third): Arrives into the box, not stationed there. Times the run so he’s invisible until the moment of delivery. The half-second between Phase 1 and Phase 2 is what beats the defender.

The headline Fortune is not a 9. He moves like a 10 and finishes like a 9.
Rex
● Half-Space Creator · Right Half-Space, the brain

The most important player in the system. Drops from the front 5 into the right half-space. Creates the triangle: Ibiso below, Jobi wide, Fortune central. Distributes, long switches to Bobby, through balls to Jobi, short combinations with Fortune.

After playing, continues his run into the box as a late arriving attacker.

Unique value Rex’s range of passing is what makes the left overload threatening. Without his switch, the left 2v1 becomes predictable and the right back gets help. Rex’s switch is what pins the right back and forces the 2v1 to be a genuine 2v1.
Jobi
● Direct Winger · Right Channel

Holds width on the right. Primary receiver from Rex’s passes. His job is simple: receive from Rex, run at the defender, beat them. Doesn’t need to create complex combinations, directness is the weapon.

When the left overload is drawing defenders, Jobi’s 1v1 opens up. Rex plays him in when the opposition’s LB has been attracted toward the centre.

Rex-Jobi partnership The pass is timed for Jobi to receive in stride going forward, not to feet standing still.
08 Back 3 & Double Pivot

The structure that lets the front 5 play.

Five attackers can only push high if there is genuine cover behind them. The back 3 plus Reuben and Ibiso are the system’s insurance, not afterthoughts, but the foundation.

Back 3 · Left
Etienne
  • Primary left-side build-up outlet.
  • Receives from GK; plays to Ibiso or directly to the left flank.
  • Does not carry beyond the halfway line.
  • Communicates with Ibiso about spacing.
Back 3 · Centre
Gabi
  • Central reference. Deepest outfield player in possession.
  • Distributes centrally when both flanks are closed.
  • Covers when Ibiso steps out.
  • Organises the back 3, calls defensive positioning.
Back 3 · Right
Mill
  • Right side of the back 3 in possession.
  • Does not advance, holds the right defensive channel.
  • If opp attacks right in transition, Mill is the first defensive line.
  • Simple passes only, to Reuben or GK.
Double Pivot · Anchor
Reuben, Screening DM
  • Positional anchor. Never leaves the space between back 3 and front 5.
  • Does not carry forward. Does not press aggressively.
  • Wins it, gives it simple, resets.
  • When Ibiso steps out, Reuben covers Ibiso’s zone.
Reuben’s one instruction If in doubt, stay.
Double Pivot · Connector
Ibiso, Progressive DM
  • The connective tissue of the system.
  • Receives from back 3, carries when space exists, releases to Rex / Fortune.
  • Reads press triggers. Covers Goody’s vacated left-back zone.
  • In transition, decides whether to counter-press or hold based on position.
Intelligence requirement Ibiso must read three things at once: where Reuben is, where Goody is, and where the ball is. His decisions make or break the system’s defensive integrity.
09 Attacking Patterns

Five patterns. Five triggers.

Patterns are not plays we run blindly. Each is triggered by a specific picture, a defender committing, the opposition pressing, the left side overloaded. Read the trigger. Run the pattern.

Pattern A

The Bobby Run

TriggerIbiso receives left side with space.
  1. Ibiso plays Bobby wide left.
  2. Bobby holds. Goody overlaps behind him.
  3. If RB stays tight on Bobby → pass to Goody behind.
  4. If RB commits to Goody → Bobby cuts inside.
  5. Fortune arrives in the box from central.
  6. Rex drifts central for any cutback.
Pattern B

The Rex Switch

TriggerDefence collapses left to deal with overload.
  1. Bobby receives wide left, draws two defenders.
  2. Bobby lays back to Ibiso or Reuben.
  3. Rex drops to right half-space, demands the ball.
  4. Quick switch to Rex.
  5. Rex plays Jobi in behind the exposed left back.
  6. Rex follows his pass into the box.
TimingRex must wait until the LB has committed before calling for the ball. The switch is only valuable after the LB has moved.
Pattern C

Fortune Late

TriggerRex has time in the right half-space.
  1. Rex receives in the right half-space.
  2. Fortune checks to feet (back to goal).
  3. Rex plays short to Fortune.
  4. Fortune lays off into Rex’s continuing run.
  5. Rex plays through ball or switches wide.
  6. Fortune turns and arrives in box for the delivery.
Pattern D

Goody Inside

TriggerBobby receives wide; the central channel opens.
  1. Bobby receives wide left.
  2. Goody makes an inside run (cuts centrally).
  3. Bobby plays inside to Goody.
  4. Goody drives at goal from central-left.
  5. Fortune drifts right to open space.
  6. Jobi’s simultaneous run pins the right CB.
  7. Goody shoots, or finds Rex arriving late.
NoteOnly works if Jobi runs simultaneously. Without his run the right CB covers Goody. Jobi’s movement is the enabler, even though he never touches the ball.
Pattern E

Playing Out vs High Press

TriggerOpposition press our back 3 with their front 2.
  1. GK plays short to Etienne or Gabi.
  2. CB draws the press, holds calmly.
  3. Ibiso has already dropped before the pass.
  4. CB plays Ibiso between the lines.
  5. Ibiso turns and plays Rex or Fortune immediately.
  6. Attack is on. Press bypassed.
CriticalIbiso’s drop must come before the ball, not after. Late drops let presses succeed.
10 Non-Negotiables

Six rules. No exceptions.

Patterns are flexible. These are not. Break any of these and the system collapses, either we lose shape, get countered, or the overloads stop appearing. Memorise them.

RULE 01
Goody & Ibiso never advance together.

When Goody pushes high on the left, Ibiso holds the half-space behind him. When Ibiso steps forward to receive, Goody stays. One up, one back, always. The left side cannot empty.

RULE 02
Bobby and Goody run opposite lines.

If Goody overlaps outside, Bobby cuts inside. If Bobby goes outside, Goody underlaps. They never run the same line. Two same-direction runs is one wasted run.

RULE 03
Reuben never leaves his zone.

Reuben is the screen. His job is the space in front of the back 3. He doesn't chase, he doesn't drift, he doesn't push up to join the press. If we lose the ball, Reuben is already where he needs to be.

RULE 04
Fortune arrives, doesn't station.

Fortune is not a stationary 10. He drops to receive, then arrives late into the box. If he stands still, the structure has no ghost runner and the box is empty when the cross comes.

RULE 05
Rex drops before the press, not during.

If Rex waits to see whether the press is coming, it's already too late. He pre-empts. He drops as the picture forms, gives Ibiso an option, and turns the press into a 3v2 between our lines.

RULE 06
Mill holds the right channel.

Mill tucks in possession to make the back 3, but the moment we lose the ball he's the right-back. He doesn't drift centrally late. The right channel is his, in transition, that channel is what protects us.

11 The Asymmetry

Two sides. Two jobs.

Wayne Tactic is deliberately uneven. The left attacks through overloads, bodies, combinations, layered runs. The right attacks through space, isolation, 1v1s, half-space passes. Same system. Different weapons.

Left Side, Overload
  • Goody pushes high. From left-back to advanced wing-back. Becomes the width.
  • Bobby is our finisher. Cuts inside, attacks the box, finishes off Goody’s service.
  • 2v1 is the weapon. Bobby + Goody vs the opposition right-back. Constant overload.
  • Fortune drifts left. Adds a third body, turns the 2v1 into a 3v2 in the half-space.
  • Combinations beat defenders. One-twos, overlaps, underlaps. Pass the opponent out of position.
vs
Right Side, Space
  • Mill tucks inside. Becomes the third centre-back. We don’t need width on the right from the back.
  • Jobi is the runner. Stays high and wide. His job is to occupy the opposition left-back, alone.
  • 1v1 + Rex is the weapon. Jobi isolated, Rex behind him in the half-space. Defenders can’t cover both.
  • Rex owns the half-space. Receives between the lines. Switches play. Plays the killer pass. Brain of the right.
  • Space beats defenders. No combinations. No overlaps. We give Jobi room and trust his quality.

The asymmetry is not an accident. It’s how we play to the strengths of the squad we have. Bobby and Goody combine well, we manufacture overloads. Jobi is direct, Rex is a thinker, we manufacture space. Defending teams have to choose: load up against the left and concede space to Jobi and Rex, or balance up and get torn apart by the 2v1.

12 Key Relationships

Four partnerships. The system runs on them.

Individuals don’t make this system work. Pairs do. These four relationships are the invisible architecture, if any one breaks, the whole shape falls apart in that zone.

Goody↔Ibiso

The balance partnership. When Goody pushes, Ibiso covers. When Ibiso advances, Goody holds. They communicate constantly, voice, eye contact, gestures. The whole left side’s defensive integrity sits on this pair reading each other.

Rex↔Fortune

The creation axis. Rex finds space in the right half-space, Fortune drifts central or left to receive. They rotate. One drops, the other arrives. Together they connect midfield to attack, without either, the front line gets isolated.

Bobby↔Goody

The 2v1 partnership. Our primary attacking weapon on the left. They never run the same line. They alternate inside-outside. The opposition right-back has no answer because every time he commits, the other one is free.

Etienne / Gabi↔Ibiso

The build-up axis. The back 3 finds Ibiso. Ibiso turns and finds the front 5. If this connection breaks, if Ibiso isn’t available, or the CBs can’t see him, we go long, we lose possession, and the entire structure goes to waste.

13 Summary at a Glance

The system, in six lines.

If you remember nothing else from this document, remember these. They are the system in its compressed form, the answer to every question a player or coach can ask about Wayne Tactic.

What we are
A 4−4−2 that becomes a 3−2−5 in possession. No striker. Goals from everywhere.
How we build
GK to back 3, back 3 to Ibiso, Ibiso to Rex or Fortune between the lines, then to the front 5. Always through the middle.
How we attack
Overload the left. Isolate Jobi on the right. Late runs from Fortune. Switches via Rex. Five lanes, always occupied.
How we defend
4−4−2 mid-block. Goody recovers to RB, Bobby and Jobi tuck. Compact. Patient. Force them wide.
How we transition
Win the ball, find Rex or Fortune between the lines, attack space. Lose the ball, Reuben screens, Goody recovers, everyone presses the ball-side. Five-second window.
The keys
Six rules. Four relationships. Two shapes. Discipline runs the system, not talent.
